The Past Tense — Прошедшее время

12 min
Teacher: Russian past tense is simpler than English — no auxiliary verbs, no "did," no "have done." Just one ending that agrees with the subject's gender. Show Mike that he already knows several past-tense forms.

The Universal Pattern — Drop -ть, Add the Ending

Take the infinitive, drop the -ть, and add one of four endings based on who did the action.
Subject Ending Example: гулять (to walk) Sample sentence
Masculine (он, я♂) -л гулял goo-LYAL Я гулял с Мабо.
I walked with Mabo. (male speaker)
Feminine (она, я♀) -ла гуляла goo-LYA-la Она гуляла с собакой.
She walked with the dog.
Neuter (оно) -ло гуляло goo-LYA-luh Солнце гуляло по небу.
The sun travelled across the sky.
Plural (мы, вы, они) -ли гуляли goo-LYA-lee Мы гуляли в парке.
We walked in the park.
Important — gender follows the speaker, not the verb: Mike says я гулял (м.) because he is male. If a woman says it, she says я гуляла (ж.). The verb tracks the person doing it.

Drill — Common Verbs You Already Know in Past Tense

Infinitiveон (m.)она (f.)они (pl.)Meaning

Notice: пошёл / пошла / пошли are slightly irregular — the masculine form drops the vowel and adds ё. That's why Пошли! ("let's go") is technically the plural past tense: "we went" = used as "let's go!"

Погода и температура — Weather & Temperature

10 min
Teacher: Mike asked Какая температура сегодня? last week — that's the perfect doorway into a fuller weather conversation. Russians say temperatures in plus/minus, not "degrees above/below zero."

How Russians Talk About Temperature

The format is always: плюс / минус + number. The word градусов (degrees) is usually dropped in conversation.

#EnglishRussianPronunciation
1 What is the temperature today? Какая сегодня температура? Ka-KA-ya see-VOHD-nyuh teem-pee-ra-TOO-ra?
2 Today it's plus ten. Сегодня плюс десять. See-VOHD-nyuh plyus DYE-syat'.
3 But there was a strong wind, so it felt much colder. Но был сильный ветер, поэтому было намного холоднее. No byl SEEL'-nyy VYE-teer, pa-EH-tah-moo BY-luh nam-NOH-guh hah-lad-NYE-yeh.
4 Tomorrow they say it will be warmer. Завтра, говорят, будет теплее. ZAHF-truh, guh-vah-RYAT, BOO-deet tep-LYE-yeh.
5 I saw smoke on the horizon. Я видел дым на горизонте. Ya VEE-deel dym na guh-ree-ZON-tye.
6 There were wildfires nearby — the air smelled smoky. Рядом были пожары — воздух был копчёный. RYA-dum BY-lee pa-ZHA-ry — VOZ-dukh byl kup-CHO-nyy.
Comparative -ee/-ей: холодно → холоднее (colder), тепло → теплее (warmer). Most weather adverbs form their comparative by adding -ее. Easy pattern!

The trick: Don't ask "what's my gender?" — ask "what's the gender of the thing I own?" Then match the ending: -й (m.), -я (f.), -ё (n.), -и (pl.).

Pошли vs Идём: Both mean "let's go," but with a flavour difference. Пошли! = "we've already started moving" (more urgent, energetic). Идём! = "we are going / let's go" (present-tense, more neutral). For Mabo at the door, both work.
